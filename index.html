01 · Foundation

What is Part-of-Speech Tagging?

Every word in a sentence plays a grammatical role. POS Tagging is the task of automatically labelling each word with its grammatical category - Noun, Verb, Adjective, etc. It's a foundational step in NLP, enabling downstream tasks like parsing, machine translation, and information extraction.

Why it matters

Without knowing word roles, a computer can't understand language structure. "I fish a fish" - the first "fish" is a verb, the second a noun. Context is everything.

The challenge

Words are ambiguous. "Can" can be a noun (tin can), a modal verb (can do), or a regular verb (can tomatoes). The surrounding words determine the correct tag.

Common Penn Treebank Tags used in this project:

NN - Noun VB - Verb JJ - Adjective RB - Adverb DT - Determiner PRP - Pronoun IN - Preposition MD - Modal

03 · The Model

How a Hidden Markov Model Works

An HMM models a sequence of hidden states (POS tags) that generate observable outputs (words). The current tag depends only on the previous tag - the Markov assumption. This simplification makes the maths tractable while still being very powerful.

P(tags | words) ∝ ∏ P(word_i | tag_i) × P(tag_i | tag_{i-1})

Hidden States (POS Tags) → Observable Words. Arrows = Transition Probabilities.

📊 Transition Matrix (A)

Learned probability that tag j follows tag i. E.g. P(NN | DT) = 0.55 - nouns very often follow determiners.

💬 Emission Matrix (B)

Probability that a specific word is produced given a tag. E.g. P("runs" | VB) is high; P("runs" | DT) is near zero.

🚀 Initial Probabilities (π)

Probability that a sentence starts with a given tag. Sentences typically begin with DT, PRP, or NNP.

🧮 Viterbi Algorithm

Dynamic programming that finds the most likely tag sequence efficiently in O(N·T²) - where T is the number of tags.

04 · Dataset

Penn Treebank Corpus

The Penn Treebank is the gold-standard dataset for POS tagging research. It contains over one million words of Wall Street Journal text, manually annotated by trained linguists.

📏 Size

~1 million words, ~50,000 sentences from WSJ financial news (1987–1989). Sections 0–18 train, 19–21 dev, 22–24 test.

🏷️ Tagset

36 POS tags + punctuation. The full PTB tagset is the de facto standard in NLP benchmarking.

🔧 NLTK Access

nltk.corpus.treebank - free 10% sample for research. Full corpus via LDC license (LDC99T42).

📈 Benchmark

A well-implemented HMM reaches ~95–96% accuracy on PTB. Our baseline (most-frequent tag) sits at ~92%.

05 · Roadmap

Plan to Build This

The project is structured into five sequential milestones. Each builds directly on the last - from raw data to a working, evaluated tagger.

01

Load & Preprocess Penn Treebank ↑ start here

Use NLTK to load tagged sentences. Extract (word, tag) pairs. Split into 80% train / 20% test. Inspect tag frequency distribution to understand the data before modelling.

02

Compute Transition Probabilities planned

Count how often tagj follows tagi across all training sentences. Normalise into probabilities. Apply Laplace (add-1) smoothing so no probability is ever zero.

03

Compute Emission Probabilities planned

Count word-given-tag occurrences, normalise. Handle unknown words (OOV) using morphological suffix rules: -ing → VBG, -ed → VBD, -ly → RB and capitalisation cues for proper nouns.

04

Implement Viterbi Decoding planned

For any new sentence, use dynamic programming to track the most probable tag at each position, then backtrack through the table to recover the full best-path sequence.

05

Evaluate & Analyse Results planned

Report token-level accuracy against PTB gold labels. Build a confusion matrix to identify which tag pairs are most ambiguous. Compare against the most-frequent-tag baseline.
